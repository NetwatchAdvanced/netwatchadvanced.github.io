The Problem

Your network is a black box.

Large businesses have dedicated security teams. Enterprise networks have monitoring tools. Your home office has a blinking light on a modem or router and a hope for the best. That gap is real and the threats are not theoretical. In this day and age, cyberattacks are no longer relegated to Enterprises or Government entities, but also at Small Office Home Office (SOHO) users and it is more important than ever to take control of your security and privacy on the internet.

No Visibility

You don't know what's on your network

Smart TVs, thermostats, doorbells, printers and more exist on your network, and unfortunately any of them can be compromised. Most home routers cannot tell you they were. A device that joined your network six months ago and has been quietly misbehaving ever since looks identical to one that's fine.

Silent Leakage

Your devices are talking without your permission

Telemetry, fingerprinting, and behavioral tracking happen constantly: from smart TVs, voice assistants, and app-connected appliances. DNS bypass attempts, where devices try to ignore your resolver and phone home directly, are becoming more and more common. Your router doesn't log it and unless configured properly, it certainly doesn't stop it.

Dead Coverage

Connectivity problems mask security ones

A slow or inconsistent network makes it impossible to tell the difference between a congestion issue, a misconfigured device, versus something actively wrong. Performance and security are not separate problems, and degraded connectivity has to be addressed before monitoring can be effective and truly mean anything.

No Entry Point

Enterprise solutions are priced out of reach

Managed security providers (MSPs) serve large businesses who have dedicated IT budgets. The SOHO market has been left with off-the-shelf gear, a setup manual nobody reads, and the assumption that home networks don't need the same level of care. But they do. Particularly with the prevalence of Work From Home/hybrid/remote style sof work, more and more home users have important assets that need to be protected, and privacy in general is increasing in priority for many people!

No Segmentation

Every device on your network trusts every other

A typical home network is flat, which means your laptop, smart TV, thermostat, and security camera all share the same network, with no boundaries between them. If one device is compromised, everything else is reachable from it and Internet of Things (IoT) devices are particularly vulnerable. SentinelOne has a solid article about IoT security here. Containment requires segmentation which most home routers do not provide, and can only be achieved through careful configuration.

No Records or Logging

When something goes wrong, there's no timeline

Most people discover a network compromise from their bank, an email provider, or a password reset that they didn't request, not from their own network and at that point the damage has already been done and you are playing catch-up! Without logging, there's no way to know when an issue started, what assets were potentially accessed, or how long this was happening. No record of course means no response.

SOHO Network Visibility

The most common threats on home and small office networks aren't sophisticated attacks. They're passive and persistent; devices that were never properly segmented, DNS queries that were never filtered, traffic that was never inspected. The damage accumulates quietly over months, not typically in dramatic incidents. For example, one TV stick on my network was creating over 1,000 DNS requests per day in attempts to send telemetry data about my equipment, network, habits and other information back home to be collected, stored, and likely sold. All without my permission, and without NetWatch I had no ability to identify these behaviors, so that I could stop that from happening.

NetWatch is a detection system, not a prevention system. It does not block traffic, stop attacks, or intervene in connections. What it does is give you visibility you don't currently have, which is a continuous record of what's happening on your network, with alerting taking place when something looks wrong.

That distinction matters. A smoke detector doesn't put out the fire, but you can't respond to a fire that you don't know is happening. Right now, most home networks have neither the detector nor the extinguisher. NetWatch is that detector and what to do with the alert is the conversation we have next.

Security Products

To be clear, consumer network security products do exist. Firewalla, Eero Secure, Netgear Armor are legitimate tools that provide a meaningful baseline for households that want something over nothing. However, they are one-size-fits all and place all of the onus on you, the user.

What they are, without exception, is products. You research them, you buy them, you install them yourself, you interpret the alerts yourself, and when something looks wrong you spend hours trying to find an accurate answer. The detection they provide is largely signature-based which means only known threats, known patterns, and known bad actors. If it's in the database, it gets flagged. If it isn't, it doesn't.

NetWatch deploys a two-layer detection model. Suricata's signature engine catches known threats against a continuously updated ruleset. The proprietary heuristic layer catches everything else, based on behavioral deviations from your network's established baseline that don't match any particular external signature and so they aren't limited in that way. A device that suddenly triples its outbound connections at 2am is flagged not because it matches a known pattern, but because it doesn't match your pattern. That behavior is truly important and is what sets NetWatch apart! It is truly a custom approach to security and privacy, that is specific to your network and activity.

It also comes with a human. Every NetWatch deployment is preceded by a connectivity and coverage audit, configured specifically for your hardware and environment, and with the appropriate Tier is supported by direct access to the operator who built and deployed the system. When you have a question, you're not opening a ticket with a support team in a timezone you've never been to, instead you're contacting the person who was in your home, knows your network, and has been helping you protect it since day one.

That combination of heuristic and signature detection, purpose-built deployment, and a direct operator relationship doesn't exist as an off-the-shelf product. It exists as a service. That's what NetWatch is, and what makes it special.
