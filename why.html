The Problem

Your network is a black box.

Large businesses have dedicated security teams. Enterprise networks have monitoring tools. Your home office has a blinking router and a hope for the best. The gap is real — and the threats are not theoretical.

No Visibility

You don't know what's on your network

Smart TVs, thermostats, doorbells, printers — any of them can be compromised. Most home routers cannot tell you they were. A device that joined your network six months ago and has been quietly misbehaving ever since looks identical to one that's fine.

Silent Leakage

Your devices are talking without your permission

Telemetry, fingerprinting, and behavioral tracking happen constantly — from smart TVs, voice assistants, and app-connected appliances. DNS bypass attempts, where devices try to ignore your resolver and phone home directly, are routine. Your router doesn't log it. It doesn't stop it.

Dead Coverage

Connectivity problems mask security ones

A slow or inconsistent network makes it impossible to tell the difference between a congestion issue, a misconfigured device, and something actively wrong. Performance and security are not separate problems — degraded connectivity has to be addressed before monitoring means anything.

No Entry Point

Enterprise solutions are priced out of reach

Managed security providers serve large businesses with dedicated IT budgets. The SOHO market has been left with off-the-shelf gear, a setup manual nobody reads, and the assumption that home networks don't need the same level of care. They do.

No Segmentation

Every device on your network trusts every other

A typical home network is flat, which means your laptop, smart TV, thermostat, and security camera all share the same network with no boundaries between them. If one device is compromised, everything else is reachable from it and Internet of Things (IoT) devices are particularly vulnerable. SentinelOne has a solid article about IoT security here. Containment requires segmentation which most home routers never provide.

No Records or Logging

When something goes wrong, there's no timeline

Most people discover a network compromise from their bank, an email provider, or a password reset they didn't request — not from their own network. Without logging, there's no way to know when it started, what was accessed, or how long it was happening. No record means no response.

The most common threats on home and small office networks aren't sophisticated attacks. They're passive and persistent; devices that were never properly segmented, DNS queries that were never filtered, traffic that was never inspected. The damage accumulates quietly over months, not typically in dramatic incidents.

NetWatch is a detection system, not a prevention system. It does not block traffic, stop attacks, or intervene in connections. What it does is give you visibility you don't currently have, which is a continuous record of what's happening on your network, with alerting when something looks wrong.

That distinction matters. A smoke detector doesn't put out the fire, but you can't respond to a fire that you don't know is happening. Right now, most home networks have neither the detector nor the extinguisher. NetWatch is that detector and what to do with the alert is the conversation we have next.
